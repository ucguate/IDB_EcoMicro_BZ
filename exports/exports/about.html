About

Approach

The Climate Risk Assessment Tool analyzes the vulnerability to climate change of the FI loan portfolio. Its use allows CUs to incorporate the analysis of climate risks into the FI’s future credit decisions, while also making recommendations for climate risk management at the client level. This tool was designed following the creation of a map of climate change risks, with specific impact on clients marked out. Training for the climate risk assessment and management tool, and on the monitoring of climate change impact of future loans, will be provided to managerial and technical staff within each of the 3 participating CUs. 

  • The Climate Risk Assessment is designed and branded based on the inputs of each CU.  
  • There is general alignment in the Climate Risk Tool design, and consistency in the approach across CUs was able to be maintained. Action Plans will vary from institution to institution based on respective Loan Application processes.  
  • There will be one virtual training delivered for all three CUs.  
  • DAI includes assumptions and sectors from both projects in creating the Climate Risk Policy. 

To be able to analyze the vulnerability of each CU’s loan portfolio, we first understood the scope of climate risks – past, present and future – for Belize and developed a tool to evaluate the existing loans. This tool can then be applied to evaluate loan applications. Where possible, DAI prioritized consistency of recommendations across the three CUs and optimized efficiencies during the development of the climate vulnerability tool. 

Overview

Climate Risk Map. DAI sourced and customized climate risk maps depicting topographic features, rivers, streams, and land use. The specificity and precision of the map depended on the availability of geographic data (topographic and locational), data on the effects of historical climatic events, and data of projected climatic risk. DAI used existing data, either through partnerships or data collection, rather than conduct climate modeling. DAI created interactive maps that will allow users to select a point and an analyze a specific location and revealing not only the zone, but map overlays of drought, fire, flood information that will help to define the severity of the risk. 

Portfolio Analysis. In order to assess the vulnerability of each CU’s loan portfolio, a series of questions were added to the Rapid Survey to define relevant climatic risks, and their severity. Questions were tailored to acknowledge known climatic risks to agriculture, fisheries and livestock within Belize to draw on more detailed insights. 

Climate Risk Tool. DAI assessed publicly available climate vulnerability tools and data with respect to BCUL and each CU’s needs. Due to COVID-19, the team was unable travel to Belize City to sit with BCUL and key CU personnel to ensure face-to-face collaboration on the tool; virtual collaborative design sessions were held with each CU. A fit-for-purpose climate risk tool was thus developed that will be relevant for all CUs, displaying the vulnerability of an existing or potential loan to climate risk, based on the location of the business or opportunity, its sector, and type of project. The tool includes an interactive map that contains layers of multi-decadal historical drought, fire, and flood climatic issues associated with the location as indicators of likely future events. It includes a series of questions that directs the user to different questions based on the location and sector of the loan under consideration. 

Training. DAI will deliver virtual training materials. We assume that BCUL will ensure that trainees from each CU participate in the training. We will train each CU’s trainers and each CU then will train its loan officers. One virtual training session will be held to provide training on the climate risk assessment tool and action plans. This session will be recorded and include a live Q&A follow-up to reinforce learning. Should it be required, our local engagement manager, Mr. Itza, will be on hand – COVID restrictions permitting, and with all government-mandated social distancing protocols observed – to provide direct user engagement support and follow-up learning by telephone or instant messaging. If urgently required, DAI will engage with BCUL and the CUs to schedule an in-person refresher session. 

Topics to be included are: 

  • Overview of Climate Change 
  • How climate change is affecting Belize  
  • How climate change can affect BCUL/CU projects  
  • Orientation to the online tool 
  • Practice Exercises. 

DAI will be available for questions that are generated by the loan officers and trainers. 

Climate Risk Policy. The climate risk policy (hereafter, “the Policy”) will be developed in consultation with BCUL and the CUs, following Beta-testing of the Tool. The Policy will outline how each CU will plan to reduce its overall exposure to climate risks. The Policy will address both acute and chronic risks from extreme weather events and changing climatic conditions. Such impacts in turn could result in financial exposure risk to CU assets, liabilities, capital and financing, in the form of lower asset values, portfolio losses and loan defaults. The Policy will outline how each CU will use the climate risk analysis to minimize both infrastructure and financial impacts to its clients’ assets in accordance with fiduciary obligations. Legal liability risks include potential litigation from parties that could suffer loss from climate change impacts such as investors, insurers or trustees. The Policy will outline when BCUL and the CUs owe which standards of care to different actors and provisions such as liability limitations and coverage clauses addressing climate risk in contractual and professional indemnity insurance documentation. Additionally, the Policy will outline when and how each CU will inform its clients and other actors regarding climate risk issues, including limitations of limitations of assumptions or advice in relation to climate risk. Finally, the Policy will establish trigger points and timetables for conducting or reassessing individual and portfolio-wide climate risk analyses.

Key elements in this task were:  

Topics to be included are: 

  • Conducting background research on the climate-related risks in each district of Belize and specific impact on agriculture, fisheries and livestock, including a review of relevant project documents already created, a literature review, and an investigation of previously developed climate vulnerability tools in the region 
  • Sourcing and customizing all openly-accessible climate vulnerability/risk maps across all six districts, including historical data for previous climatic events (drought, wildfire, floods) 
  • Analyzing the vulnerability of the existing loan portfolio of each CU 
  • Developing a climate vulnerability tool for future loans  
  • Practice Exercises. 

Remaining elements in this task are:  

  • Training BCUL and CUs on how to use the tool  
  • Developing each CU’s climate risk policy
